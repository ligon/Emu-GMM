* Introduction and Population Primitives

The framework is named for the operator at its centre: =emu= reads as \(\mathbb{E}_\mu\), the expectation under a measure \(\mu\). The central design claim is that one operator interface --- implemented against empirical, analytical, or synthetic measures --- drives sample estimation, identification analysis, and simulation-based inference through a single computational pipeline.

The most important architectural commitment is that variance construction is orthogonal to integration: \(\mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi]\) is a property of the measure; \(V_\mu(\theta)\) is a property of a separate =CovarianceStrategy= that can be swapped (iid vs cluster-robust vs replicate-weight) without changing what is being integrated. The same =EmpiricalMeasure= drives any covariance strategy on the v1/v2 design-awareness ladder.

This document covers the interface algebra (Section 2), a worked Euler example (Section 3), differentiation (Section 4), estimation and inference (Section 5), and named-tensor boundary tooling (Section 6).

** Population Primitives
Let \(\Theta \subset \mathbb{R}^K\) denote a compact parameter space. The framework's underlying stochastic object is a structural residual function \(\psi: \mathcal{X} \times \Theta \to \mathbb{R}^M\), measurable in its first argument, evaluated at observations drawn from a population measure \(\mu_\text{pop}\) on \((\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{F})\). The model structure follows from these derived mappings:

- Parameter Vector :: \(\theta \in \Theta\), where \(\theta_0\) represents the unique true parameter vector.
- Population Moment Function :: \(m: \Theta \to \mathbb{R}^M\), the population expectation of \(\psi\):
  \[ m(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\mu_\text{pop}}[\psi(\cdot, \theta)] \]
  The fundamental identification restriction is \(m(\theta_0) = 0\). Sections 2-5 generalize from \(\mu_\text{pop}\) to whatever measure the user supplies.
- Variance-Covariance Mapping :: \(V(\theta)\), the \(M \times M\) population covariance matrix of \(\psi\) under \(\mu_\text{pop}\):
  \[ V(\theta) = \operatorname{Var}_{\mu_\text{pop}}[\psi(\cdot, \theta)] = \mathbb{E}_{\mu_\text{pop}}[\psi\,\psi'] - m(\theta)\, m(\theta)' \]
  Assumed positive definite at \(\theta_0\). /Sample-level convention/: \(V_X(\theta)\) estimates the variance of the sample moment /estimator/ \(\bar m_X(\theta)\) rather than the per-observation variance of \(\psi\); the \(1/N\) scaling is absorbed into \(V_X\) throughout (see /Architectural Core Highlights/ in Section 5).
- Optimal Weighting Matrix :: \(\Lambda(\theta) = V^{-1}(\theta)\), providing the metric for the parameter space.
- Population Jacobian Matrix :: \(G(\theta)\), the \(M \times K\) matrix of first derivatives:
  \[ G(\theta) = \frac{\partial m(\theta)}{\partial \theta'} \]

** Local Geometry and Information Bounds
The population criterion function \(Q(\theta)\) is defined as the quadratic form:
\[ Q(\theta) = m(\theta)' \Lambda(\theta) m(\theta) \]
By construction, \(Q(\theta) \geq 0\) for all \(\theta \in \Theta\), achieving its global minimum at \(Q(\theta_0) = 0\).

Taking the second derivative of \(Q(\theta)\) and evaluating it at the truth \(\theta_0\), the terms containing the first and second derivatives of \(\Lambda(\theta)\) as well as the second derivatives of \(m(\theta)\) vanish because \(m(\theta_0) = 0\). The population Hessian collapses precisely to:
\[ \nabla^2 Q(\theta_0) = 2 G(\theta_0)' \Lambda(\theta_0) G(\theta_0) = 2 \mathcal{I}(\theta_0) \]
where \(\mathcal{I}(\theta_0)\) is the asymptotic precision of efficient GMM for this moment system --- loosely, the GMM information matrix:
\[ \mathcal{I}(\theta_0) = G(\theta_0)' \Lambda(\theta_0) G(\theta_0) \]
This is the efficient-GMM analogue of the Cramér-Rao bound; it coincides with the latter only when the moments span the score and the data-generating process is correctly specified.

* Mathematical Interfaces and Operational Decoupling

The architecture separates the model into orthogonal interfaces so that integration, variance construction, and structural specification can be substituted independently:

- =StructuralModel= --- the economic primitives (residuals and their gradients at a single observation).
- =Measure= --- an integration operator \(\mu\) over \((\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{F})\) exposing \(\mathbb{E}_\mu[\,\cdot\,]\).
- =CovarianceStrategy= --- a constructor that maps pointwise \(\psi\) values to the \(M \times M\) matrix \(V_\mu(\theta)\).
- =Context= --- a convenience bundler that ships a (measure, strategy) pair appropriate to a given modality.

=CovarianceStrategy= is parallel to (not nested under) =Measure= at the type level. Estimation is invoked as \(\text{Estimator}(\text{model},\, \text{measure},\, \text{strategy},\, \text{weighting})\) rather than \(\text{Estimator}(\text{model},\, \text{context})\); the =Context= type exists purely as sugar. This matters because the choice of variance construction is orthogonal to the choice of integration: a single =EmpiricalMeasure= can be paired with iid, cluster-robust, or replicate-weight variance strategies without changing the measure object, and the v1/v2 design-awareness ladder lives entirely on the strategy side.

** Primitives

*** The Model Primitive: StructuralModel
The =StructuralModel= abstraction encapsulates only the elementary, observation-level structural equations. It possesses no awareness of sample scale, sampling weights, missingness patterns, or the empirical design layout.

- *Elementary Function*: \(\psi(x, \theta)\), an \(M \times 1\) vector mapping an observation \(x \in \mathcal{X} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^D\) and parameter vector \(\theta \in \Theta\) to structural residuals (e.g., the raw Euler equation residual for an individual household).
- *Elementary Gradient*: \(\nabla_\theta \psi(x, \theta) = \frac{\partial \psi(x, \theta)}{\partial \theta'}\), an \(M \times K\) matrix of structural derivatives evaluated at a single point.

*** The Integration Primitive: Measure
The =Measure= interface (\(\mu\)) acts strictly as a functional integration operator over a measurable space \((\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{F})\). It is agnostic to whether \(\mu\) is composed of real-world survey responses, analytical density functions, or synthetic simulations, and exposes exactly two operations when evaluated against a =StructuralModel= at parameter vector \(\theta\):

- Expectation Mapping ::
   \[ \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi] = \int_\mathcal{X} \psi(x, \theta) \, d\mu(x) \]
- Jacobian Integration ::
   \[ \mathbb{E}_\mu[\nabla_\theta \psi] = \int_\mathcal{X} \nabla_\theta \psi(x, \theta) \, d\mu(x) \]

The =Measure= is a /computational interface/, not a strict measure-theoretic object. Implementations may glue together coordinate-specific measures or other internal machinery as long as the interface contract is honored. Whether a particular implementation corresponds to a single \(\sigma\)-finite measure or, as in the empirical case below, an \(M\)-tuple of coordinate-specific measures sharing a support, is an implementation detail invisible to the rest of the framework. The architecture is unified by polymorphism over the interface, not by mathematical structure of the underlying measures.

Variance construction is /not/ a responsibility of the =Measure= --- it lives in =CovarianceStrategy=.

*** The Variance Primitive: CovarianceStrategy
The =CovarianceStrategy= interface (\(\Sigma\)) is responsible for constructing the \(M \times M\) covariance matrix \(V_\mu(\theta)\) of the sample moment vector. It consumes pointwise \(\psi(x_i, \theta)\) evaluations (for empirical and synthetic measures) or analytic integrands (for analytical measures), and produces \(V_\mu(\theta)\).

Separating variance construction from integration isolates procedural and design concerns --- clustering, stratification, replicate weighting, missingness handling, regularization --- entirely outside the core =Measure= definition. The same =EmpiricalMeasure= can be combined with multiple strategies; the strategy doesn't change what \(\mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi]\) is, only what \(V_\mu(\theta)\) is.

*** The Context Bundle
For a given modality (Empirical / Analytical / Synthetic), the natural pairing of a =Measure= with a default =CovarianceStrategy= is exposed as a =Context= type. A =Context= holds:
- the measure \(\mu\);
- a default covariance strategy \(\Sigma_\mu\);
- any modality-specific metadata (cluster IDs, replicate weights, frozen simulation draws, quadrature grids) consumed by the strategy.

The estimation API accepts either an unbundled =(measure, strategy)= pair or a =Context=. =Context= is sugar, not a third primitive --- everything it does is delegation to its components.

** Polymorphic Implementations

*** EmpiricalMeasure
This implementation maps to a physical dataset. Strictly speaking, \(\mu_X\) is not a single scalar measure but an \(M\)-tuple of coordinate-specific measures \(\{\mu_X^{(j)}\}_{j=1}^M\) sharing a common support; the matrix-valued shorthand below packages this \(M\)-tuple into a single expression.

The measure is represented as a discrete, empirical mass:
\[ d\mu_X = \mathbf{N}_X \sum_{i \in X} D_i w_i \delta_{x_i} \]
where \(\delta_{x_i}\) is a Dirac delta at observation \(x_i\); \(w_i = 1/\pi_i\) is the probability weight (inverse inclusion probability --- /pweight/ semantics, not aweight or fweight); and \(D_i = \operatorname{diag}(d_i)\) is an \(M \times M\) coordinate-masking matrix tracking missingness per moment coordinate (\(d_{ij} = 1\) if moment \(j\) is observable for unit \(i\), \(0\) otherwise).

The dimension-specific scales are managed by the \(M \times M\) diagonal matrix \(\mathbf{N}_X\):
\[ \mathbf{N}_X = \operatorname{diag}\left( \frac{1}{N_1}, \frac{1}{N_2}, \dots, \frac{1}{N_M} \right) \quad \text{where} \quad N_j = \sum_{i \in X} d_{ij} \]

Reading coordinate-wise:
\[ \mathbb{E}_{\mu_X}[\psi]_j = \frac{1}{N_j} \sum_{i \in X} d_{ij} w_i\, \psi_j(x_i, \theta), \]
each moment is integrated against its own coordinate-specific measure, with missing observations dropped per coordinate rather than per row.

*** Empirical Covariance Strategies
The design-awareness ladder lives here. The =EmpiricalMeasure= can be paired with any of the following:

- =IIDCovariance=: pweighted iid form with pairwise-overlap missingness handling. The element \((j,k)\) uses the rows where both \(\psi_j\) and \(\psi_k\) are observed:
  \[ [V_X(\theta)]_{jk} = \frac{1}{N_j N_k} \sum_{i \in X} d_{ij} d_{ik}\, w_i^2\, \psi_j(x_i, \theta)\, \psi_k(x_i, \theta) \]
  Single-stage SRS with optional pweights; the common case.

- =ClusteredCovariance(cluster_ids)=: cluster totals before outer product. With clusters indexed by \(c\):
  \[ [V_X(\theta)]_{jk} = \frac{1}{N_j N_k} \sum_c \Big(\sum_{i \in c} d_{ij} w_i\, \psi_j(x_i, \theta)\Big) \Big(\sum_{i \in c} d_{ik} w_i\, \psi_k(x_i, \theta)\Big) \]
  Required when within-cluster correlation matters (households within villages, students within schools). The =IIDCovariance= form is the special case where each cluster is a single observation.

- /v2 planned/: =StratifiedCovariance= (stratification with multiple PSUs per stratum); =ReplicateWeightCovariance= (BRR, jackknife, bootstrap variants for federal-survey datasets that distribute design information as replicate weight columns).

- /Out of scope/: single-PSU strata collapse rules; finite-population corrections; calibration / raking weights whose values depend on the realized sample.

Both =IIDCovariance= and =ClusteredCovariance= use /pairwise overlap/ (a.k.a. /available-pairs/) to avoid listwise deletion: each \([V_X]_{jk}\) uses every row that supports its pair, not just rows complete on every moment. The cost is that \(V_X\) is not guaranteed positive definite in finite samples --- see the regularization policy in Section 5.

*** EmpiricalContext
Bundles an =EmpiricalMeasure= with a default =CovarianceStrategy= (=ClusteredCovariance= when cluster IDs are present in the metadata, =IIDCovariance= otherwise) plus the dataset itself.

A structural property worth highlighting: \(\mathbb{E}_{\mu_X}[\psi]\) is design-invariant across every covariance strategy on the ladder. Only \(V_X(\theta)\) changes. The residual path through the estimator therefore needs no design-specific code; specialization lives entirely inside =CovarianceStrategy=.

*** AnalyticalMeasure
Contains no empirical data matrix. Represents the true continuous or discrete population distribution governed by the economic environment (e.g., joint log-normal income and preference shocks). Integration is performed via numerical quadrature (Gauss-Hermite, Gauss-Legendre) or exact closed-form solutions where available.

*** AnalyticalCovariance
Computes \(V(\theta)\) directly from the population density \(f\):
\[ V(\theta) = \int_\mathcal{X} \psi(x, \theta)\psi(x, \theta)'\, f(x) \, dx - \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi]\, \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi]' \]
typically via the same quadrature grid used by the measure.

*** AnalyticalContext
Bundles the =AnalyticalMeasure= and =AnalyticalCovariance= for an exhaustive specification of the true data-generating process. This pairing is the workhorse of the identification-verification diagnostic discussed in Section 5: at the known true value \(\theta_0\) the theoretical criterion satisfies \(Q(\theta_0) = 0\) exactly, and the numerical optimizer should converge to \(\theta_0\) to within quadrature and floating-point tolerance.

*** SyntheticMeasure
A stochastic parametric simulator whose law depends on \(\theta\). Three design choices keep it well-behaved:

1. *Common Random Numbers (CRN)*: the measure holds a frozen batch of \(N_{sim}\) latent draws \(\{z_s\}\) (a fixed =PRNGKey= in JAX) and recomputes observations \(x_s(\theta) = f(z_s; \theta)\) from the /same/ \(z_s\) at each \(\theta\). This converts a stochastic objective into a deterministic surface in \(\theta\), restoring the smoothness gradient-based optimisers require. Fresh draws at every evaluation produce \(\theta\)-varying Monte Carlo noise that defeats both quasi-Newton and trust-region methods.
2. *Reparameterization for derivatives*: when \(f(z; \theta)\) is smooth in \(\theta\) and \(z\) comes from a \(\theta\)-free base distribution, the Leibniz exchange holds inside the simulator:
   \[ \nabla_\theta \mathbb{E}_z[\psi(f(z;\theta), \theta)] = \mathbb{E}_z[\nabla_\theta \psi(f(z;\theta), \theta)] \]
   and JAX propagates the gradient end-to-end. For distributions that cannot be reparameterised (discrete latents, \(\theta\)-dependent support), the framework supports score-function (REINFORCE) gradients as an opt-in fallback, with the variance caveats that implies.
3. *Simulation cardinality*: \(N_{sim}\) is exposed as part of the =SyntheticMeasure='s state; the inference engine consumes it (alongside the empirical \(N\)) to assemble the canonical \((1 + 1/S)\) SMM inflation factor where \(S = N_{sim}/N\).

*** SyntheticCovariance
The variance of the simulated moment estimator \(\bar m_{sim}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\mu_\text{sim}(\theta)}[\psi]\), scaled consistently with the empirical-side convention (variance of the moment estimator, not of an individual draw). For iid simulation draws:
\[ V_\text{sim}(\theta) = \frac{1}{N_{sim}}\Big( \mathbb{E}_z[\psi\, \psi'] - \mathbb{E}_z[\psi]\, \mathbb{E}_z[\psi]' \Big) \]
When combined additively with \(V_X\) in a matching SMM estimator, the result yields the standard \((1+1/S)\) inflation factor mechanically, without a separate correction.

*** SyntheticContext
Bundles =SyntheticMeasure= and =SyntheticCovariance= with the frozen \(z_s\) batch and any auxiliary metadata (e.g., the choice of reparameterization function). When combined with an =EmpiricalContext= in a matching estimator, this yields a Simulated Method of Moments estimator [cite:@mcfadden89;@pakes-pollard89] that is differentiable, JIT-compilable, and design-aware on the empirical side.

** Architectural Utility of Data-Free Measures
Decoupling the measure from physical data files introduces major diagnostic and theoretical capabilities at the design level:

1. *Deterministic Identification Verifications*: A =StructuralModel= can be integrated over an =AnalyticalContext= parameterized at a known true value \(\theta_0\). The analytical moment condition satisfies \(\int \psi(x, \theta_0) \, d\mu_\text{analytical} = 0\) exactly; numerically, the optimizer should converge to \(\theta_0\) within quadrature and floating-point precision. Failures beyond that tolerance isolate a global identification failure or an ill-behaved objective surface, independent of sample noise --- see the diagnostics taxonomy in Section 5.
2. *Ex-Ante Efficiency Mapping*: Evaluating the system curvature using an =AnalyticalContext= yields the true population information matrix \(\mathcal{I}(\theta_0) = G(\theta_0)' \Lambda(\theta_0) G(\theta_0)\). This enables the programmatic evaluation of alternative moment configurations (\(\psi\)) to maximize the information envelope before field data is ever introduced.

* Worked Example --- Consumption Euler Equation

To demonstrate the abstraction concretely, consider a standard consumption Euler equation under power utility. The model parameters are \(\theta = (\beta, \gamma) \in \Theta \subset \mathbb{R}^2\) where \(\beta\) is the discount factor and \(\gamma\) is the coefficient of relative risk aversion. The observation vector is \(x_t = (c_t, c_{t+1}, r_{t+1}) \in \mathbb{R}^3\): current consumption, realised next-period consumption, and the realised asset return.

** The StructuralModel
The elementary Euler-equation residual is:
\[ \psi(x_t, \theta) = \beta \left( \frac{c_{t+1}}{c_t} \right)^{-\gamma} (1 + r_{t+1}) - 1 \]

With \(M = 1\) and \(K = 2\), the analytical \(M \times K\) Jacobian is the \(1 \times 2\) row:
\[ \nabla_\theta \psi(x_t, \theta) = \begin{bmatrix} \left( \tfrac{c_{t+1}}{c_t} \right)^{-\gamma} (1 + r_{t+1}) & -\beta \left( \tfrac{c_{t+1}}{c_t} \right)^{-\gamma} (1 + r_{t+1}) \ln\!\left( \tfrac{c_{t+1}}{c_t} \right) \end{bmatrix} \]

The =StructuralModel= holds these two functions and nothing else --- no data, no quadrature grids, no simulation seeds.

Three estimation paths, one =StructuralModel=:

- /Empirical/: \(\hat\theta = \arg\min_\theta Q_X(\theta)\) on CEX household data with cluster-robust variance.
- /Analytical/: the theoretical \(\arg\min_\theta Q_\text{analytical}(\theta)\) is \(\theta_0\) with \(Q = 0\) exactly; numerically, deviations beyond quadrature tolerance diagnose identification or conditioning failures, not sample noise.
- /Synthetic/: simulate \(\{(c_t, c_{t+1}, r_{t+1})\}_{t=1}^{N_\text{sim}}\) from a generative model parameterised at \(\theta\), hand to the same estimator paired with the empirical context. SMM falls out.

The same =psi=, the same gradient, the same objective: only the =Context= changes.

* Differentiation Architecture and Gradient Propagation

A critical practical challenge in executing structural estimation is the efficient and accurate propagation of derivatives through the model components. Because the objective function is minimized using gradient-based numerical optimization, and because the computation of the sample information matrix requires the evaluation of the population Jacobian, differentiation must be seamlessly wired across the code blocks.

Rather than treating differentiation as a global monolithic process, this architecture isolates automatic differentiation (AD) at the individual observation level while leveraging the linearity of the =Measure= abstraction to handle aggregation.

** The Computational Boundary for Automatic Differentiation
Running reverse-mode or forward-mode automatic differentiation over a massive empirical dataset can lead to catastrophic memory explosion (graph bloat) if the tracking engine logs every individual observation loop, sampling weight multiplication, and cluster-level accumulation.

To circumvent this, the AD graph is strictly isolated at the element-wise boundary:

- Observation-Level Graphing :: The system computes the structural error \(\psi(x, \theta)\) and its local gradient \(\nabla_\theta \psi(x, \theta)\) at a single point \(x\).
- Vectorized Batches (Vmap) :: For efficient hardware execution (e.g., SIMD lanes or GPU execution), the observation-level execution graph is batched across points using single-instruction, multiple-data vectorization primitives.
- Graph Detachment :: The tracking graph for automatic differentiation terminates immediately after delivering the pointwise matrices. The subsequent linear operations --- applying masking indicators (\(D_i\)) and structural weights (\(w_i\)) --- are executed via standard native tensor math, bypassing the AD overhead entirely.

This isolation strategy presumes the measure \(\mu\) itself does not depend on \(\theta\). For =EmpiricalMeasure= and =AnalyticalMeasure= with fixed quadrature grids this holds exactly. For =SyntheticMeasure=, where the law of the draws depends on \(\theta\), the isolation is maintained at the level of the latent variates: the frozen \(z_s\) batch carries no AD graph, while \(f(z_s; \theta)\) and \(\psi(f(z_s;\theta), \theta)\) are traced as usual.

** The Linearity of Gradient Integration
By the Leibniz Integral Rule, under standard regularity conditions allowing the exchange of differentiation and integration, the Jacobian mapping of the measure is equivalent to the integrated expectation of the local gradients.

For any valid =Measure= \(\mu\) whose support does not depend on \(\theta\), the functional interface maps the derivative through the integration space linearly:
\[ G_\mu(\theta) = \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta'} \int \psi(x, \theta) \, d\mu(x) = \int \left[ \nabla_\theta \psi(x, \theta) \right] \, d\mu(x) \]

- In an =AnalyticalMeasure=, this maps to the direct integration of the analytical derivative mapping over the continuous density.
- In an =EmpiricalMeasure=, this maps to the coordinate-scaled matrix product:
  \[ G_X(\theta) = \mathbf{N}_X \sum_{i \in X} D_i w_i \left[ \nabla_\theta \psi(x_i, \theta) \right] \]
- In a =SyntheticMeasure= with reparameterised draws, Leibniz applies in the latent space: \(G_\mu(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_z[\nabla_\theta \psi(f(z;\theta), \theta)]\), including the contribution that flows through \(f\).

** Differentiating through the Continuously Updating Weighting Matrix
Because this system targets the optimal GMM framework where the weighting matrix can be treated as a continuous function of the parameter vector itself, evaluating the full gradient of the criterion function requires differentiating through a matrix dependency.

Let \(Q_\mu(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi]' V_\mu^{-1}(\theta) \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi]\). The exact analytical gradient with respect to a scalar parameter \(\theta_k\) is:
\[ \frac{\partial Q_\mu(\theta)}{\partial \theta_k} = 2 \left[ \frac{\partial \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi]}{\partial \theta_k} \right]' V_\mu^{-1}(\theta) \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi] + \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi]' \left[ \frac{\partial V_\mu^{-1}(\theta)}{\partial \theta_k} \right] \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi] \]

Using the matrix derivative identity for an inverted matrix, the derivative of the optimal weighting metric maps directly back to the derivative of the variance:
\[ \frac{\partial V_\mu^{-1}(\theta)}{\partial \theta_k} = -V_\mu^{-1}(\theta) \left[ \frac{\partial V_\mu(\theta)}{\partial \theta_k} \right] V_\mu^{-1}(\theta). \]
Substituting yields the complete gradient vector:
\[ \nabla_\theta Q_\mu(\theta) = 2 G_\mu(\theta)' V_\mu^{-1}(\theta) \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi] - \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi]' V_\mu^{-1}(\theta) \left[ \nabla_\theta V_\mu(\theta) \right] V_\mu^{-1}(\theta) \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi] \]

*** Asymptotic Equivalence of CU and Iterated Weighting
At the true parameter values (\(\theta_0\) in the population, or asymptotically at \(\hat{\theta}\)), \(\mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi(\cdot, \theta_0)] \xrightarrow{p} 0\). The second term in the gradient is a quadratic form in the moment vector and vanishes at rate \(O_p(N^{-1})\), while the primary term vanishes at \(O_p(N^{-1/2})\):
\[ \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi]' V_\mu^{-1}(\theta) \left[ \nabla_\theta V_\mu(\theta) \right] V_\mu^{-1}(\theta) \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi] \xrightarrow{p} 0 \]
This is the theoretical justification for why the /Iterated/ weighting strategy (which fixes \(V\) during each inner solve and refreshes it between iterations, so its inner gradient never sees \(\nabla_\theta V\)) converges to the same fixed point as the /Continuously Updated/ strategy (which sees \(\nabla_\theta V\) at every evaluation). It is *not* a license to drop \(\nabla_\theta V\) from the CU gradient implementation: away from the optimum the dropped term is \(O_p(1)\), and silently omitting it produces an estimator that is neither CU nor Iterated.

The clean architectural resolution --- see Section 5 --- is to make weighting an explicit strategy choice and to write the residual function so that JAX's AD picks up exactly the right gradient for each strategy.

For the information matrix used by the inference engine, \(\mathcal{I}_\mu(\hat\theta) = G_\mu(\hat\theta)' \Lambda_\mu(\hat\theta) G_\mu(\hat\theta)\) --- computed directly from \(G\) and \(\Lambda\), not from a numerical Hessian of \(Q\). The asymptotic argument above implies the two coincide at \(\hat\theta\); in finite samples they need not, and the direct \(G'\Lambda G\) form is both cheaper and more stable.

* Estimation, Decomposition, and Specification Testing

** The Decomposition-Driven Criterion, Q_\mu(\theta)
To ensure numerical stability and prevent precision loss from explicit matrix inversion, =emu-gmm= avoids calculating \(V_\mu^{-1}(\theta)\) directly. Instead, it operates on the lower-triangular Cholesky factorization of the design-aware covariance matrix:
\[ V_\mu(\theta) = L_\mu(\theta) L_\mu(\theta)' \]
The system computes a whitened moment vector \(y_\mu(\theta)\) via forward substitution, avoiding the direct inversion of the system:
\[ L_\mu(\theta) y_\mu(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi(\cdot, \theta)] \]
The objective function is then evaluated strictly as the squared Euclidean \(L_2\) norm of this transformed vector:
\[ Q_\mu(\theta) = y_\mu(\theta)' y_\mu(\theta) = \| y_\mu(\theta) \|^2_2 \]
The GMM estimator is defined as:
\[ \hat{\theta} = \arg\min_{\theta \in \Theta} Q_\mu(\theta) \]
Framing the objective as a pure sum-of-squares allows the system to bypass general-purpose quasi-Newton optimizers and interface directly with high-performance non-linear least-squares solvers (Levenberg-Marquardt, trust-region reflective). These solvers exploit the structure to approximate local updates efficiently using only first derivatives. JAX provides exactly these derivatives through the residual function \(y_\mu(\theta)\), including the dependence of \(L_\mu\) on \(\theta\) when the weighting strategy calls for it (see below).

** The Weighting Strategy Interface
The choice of weighting policy is encoded as an explicit object that the optimization loop consults at every evaluation. Four strategies cover the standard cases:

- =Identity=: \(\Lambda_\mu(\theta) \equiv I_M\). Useful as a first stage when no preliminary \(V\) is available, or for diagnostic runs.
- =Fixed(V0)=: two-step GMM. The user supplies a precomputed \(V_0\) (typically from a first-stage =Identity= run); \(\Lambda\) is held fixed throughout the second-stage optimization.
- =Iterated(k_max, tol)=: iterated GMM. Alternates between solving \(\hat\theta_k = \arg\min_\theta\, \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi]'\, V_\mu(\hat\theta_{k-1})^{-1}\, \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi]\) and refreshing \(V\) at the new \(\hat\theta_k\), up to convergence or \(k_{\max}\) rounds. Each inner solve is a =Fixed= problem; gradients within an iteration are exact.
- =ContinuouslyUpdated= (default): \(\Lambda_\mu(\theta)\) is recomputed at every \(\theta\) evaluation. The residual \(y_\mu(\theta) = L_\mu(\theta)^{-1} \mathbb{E}_\mu[\psi(\cdot,\theta)]\) depends on \(\theta\) through both factors; the NLLS Jacobian computed by JAX captures this dependence exactly, without manual derivation of the \(\nabla_\theta V\) term from Section 4.

A strategy is a JAX pytree carrying its state (precomputed \(V_0\), iteration counter, etc.) so it composes cleanly with =jit= and =vmap=. Section 4's asymptotic-equivalence result guarantees =Iterated= and =ContinuouslyUpdated= share the same limit; the choice between them is a matter of finite-sample behavior and computational cost rather than identification.

** Regularization of \(V_\mu(\theta)\)
When an =EmpiricalCovarianceStrategy= constructs \(V_X(\theta)\) via the pairwise-overlap rule, the result is not guaranteed positive definite in finite samples. The Cholesky pipeline requires \(V_\mu \succ 0\), so the framework applies adaptive regularization:
\[ V_\mu^\star(\theta) = V_\mu(\theta) + \tau\, \operatorname{diag}(V_\mu(\theta)) \]
with \(\tau\) chosen to enforce a target condition number ceiling (default \(\kappa_\text{target} = 10^6\)). The diagonal-Tikhonov form is scale-equivariant under per-moment rescaling, which matters because moment functions are routinely on heterogeneous scales (a residual in dollars and a residual in shares should not see the same absolute ridge).

Alternative regularisers are available as opt-in:
- *Eigenvalue floor*: clip eigenvalues at \(\epsilon\,\lambda_{\max}(V_\mu)\) via spectral decomposition. Transparent; \(O(M^3)\).
- *Higham nearest-PSD* [cite:@higham88]: project to the nearest positive semidefinite matrix in Frobenius norm. Most principled, slowest.

To avoid introducing non-smoothness into the objective surface, \(\tau\) is selected once at an anchor point (first-stage \(\hat\theta_1\) for =Fixed= / =Iterated=; a pilot evaluation for =ContinuouslyUpdated=) and held fixed thereafter. The chosen \(\tau\), the achieved \(\kappa(V_\mu^\star)\), and \(\lambda_{\min}(V_\mu)/\lambda_{\max}(V_\mu)\) are logged at every \(V\) refresh; a binding ridge is surfaced as a diagnostic flag rather than silently absorbed.

Regularization alters the limit law of \(Q_\mu(\hat\theta)\): the \(\chi^2_{M-K}\) distribution becomes a weighted-\(\chi^2\) whose weights depend on \(\tau\). For non-binding \(\tau\) the deviation is negligible; the inference engine reports both the nominal \(\chi^2\) critical value and a regularization-adjusted critical value when \(\tau\) exceeds a configurable threshold (default \(\tau > 0.01\)).

** Identification Diagnostics
When a =StructuralModel= is integrated against an =AnalyticalContext= parameterised at a known true value \(\theta_0\), the population moment condition \(\mathbb{E}_{\mu_\text{analytical}}[\psi(\cdot,\theta_0)] = 0\) holds exactly. The framework leverages this to provide 

1. *Local boundary pathology* --- gradient zero, objective positive. \(\nabla_\theta Q(\theta^\star) = 0\) but \(Q(\theta^\star) > 0\) at the reported solution \(\theta^\star \neq \theta_0\). Interpretation: local identification failure or multi-modal global non-identification; the moment system has a stationary point that is not the truth.
2. *Conditioning pathology* --- Cholesky pivot failure. The factorization \(V_\mu(\theta) = L_\mu(\theta) L_\mu(\theta)'\) encounters a zero or sub-tolerance pivot. Interpretation: moment redundancy or collinearity; the framework reports the column index at which the pivot failed, mapping it back to the offending moment in the =StructuralModel=. Distinct from the pairwise-overlap regularization case above: a pivot failure on an =AnalyticalContext= is a structural identification problem, not a sampling artifact.
3. *Numerical instability* --- gradient nonzero at the known root. \(\nabla_\theta Q(\theta_0) \neq 0\) when evaluated at the supplied truth. Interpretation: quadrature insufficiency, severe parameter scale disparities, or floating-point underflow --- solver mechanics rather than theory.

Each diagnostic surfaces a structured report (failure category, offending dimension index, residual magnitude) rather than a generic optimization failure. Combined with the analytical-context round-trip, this transforms identification analysis from an /a priori/ derivation into an /a posteriori/ test that runs against the actual implementation.

** The Overidentification Statistic
Under the null hypothesis that the parametric model is correctly specified, and with \(V_\mu(\theta)\) used unregularized, the minimized sample criterion function converges cleanly to a chi-squared distribution:
\[ Q_X(\hat{\theta}) \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{M - K}. \]
When the variance has been regularized, the limit is a weighted sum of chi-squared variates; the inference engine reports both the nominal and regularization-adjusted critical values as described in the regularization section above.

** Architectural Core Highlights
1. *No External Scalar Multipliers*: Because \(V_\mu(\theta)\) represents the variance of the sample moment vector itself (scaling at \(O(1/N)\)) rather than the variance of an individual observation, the standard textbook \(N\) multiplier is absorbed into the variance definition; \(Q_X(\hat{\theta})\) converges to a \(\chi^2\) natively. This is a convention choice rather than a free lunch --- it differs from the [cite/t:@hansen82] / Hayashi textbook normalization, and the divergence should be remembered when porting code from elsewhere.
2. *Asymmetric Scale Resolution*: The interaction of the coordinate scaling inside \(G_\mu(\theta)\) and the scaling inside \(V_\mu(\theta)\) ensures that the sample information matrix properly weighs the relative data density across different variables without requiring listwise deletion.

** Open Theoretical Questions
The pairwise-overlap covariance machinery introduced in Section 2 sidesteps listwise deletion at the cost of theoretical loose ends that deserve treatment before the framework is leaned on for primary inference:

1. *Asymptotic theory under per-coordinate missingness.* The classical GMM consistency and asymptotic-normality results assume the moment vector \(\bar m_X(\theta)\) is the sample mean of an iid (or appropriately mixing) sequence. Under per-coordinate missingness with selection indicators \(d_{ij}\), the relevant sequence depends on the missingness mechanism. MCAR (selection independent of \(\theta\) and \(\psi\)) carries the standard results through with \(V\) rescaled by per-coordinate effective sample sizes; MAR and MNAR require additional structure. The framework currently makes no claim about consistency outside MCAR.

2. *Asymptotic target of \(V_X(\theta)\) under the pairwise-overlap rule.* The per-coordinate normalization \(1/(N_j N_k)\) produces a consistent estimator of \(\operatorname{Var}[\bar m_X(\theta)]\) under MCAR with bounded inclusion probabilities. The general case requires showing that overlap fractions \(N_{jk}/N\) converge to positive limits --- a condition we can state but have not verified across all design types on the v1/v2 ladder.

3. *Finite-sample versus asymptotic positive-definiteness.* The diagonal-Tikhonov regularization handles finite-sample PD failure but distorts the J-statistic's limit law. The asymptotic PD of \(V_X(\theta_0)\) under standard conditions (MCAR, bounded weights, finite fourth moments) is straightforward; the rate at which finite-sample PD becomes reliable in practice is not characterized here.

Items (1) and (2) under MCAR --- plus the J-statistic limit and asymptotic positive-definiteness of \(V^\text{lim}\) --- are established in the companion note =docs/mcar-asymptotics.org=. The remaining quantitative and MAR/MNAR pieces are deferred to later revisions of that note. The implementation paths described in this document are unchanged by any of this: the open items affect what we /claim/ the estimator is doing, not what the code does.

* Boundary Interfaces, Named Tensors, and PyTree Topology

To maintain mathematical legibility without introducing non-compilable runtime bottlenecks, =emu-gmm= eschews traditional dataframe paradigms (like =pandas=) inside the computation zone. Instead, the framework utilizes JAX-native named tensor architectures (=haliax=) and type-level axis verification (=jaxtyping=).

This ensures that labels, dimension names, and missingness masks are fully integrated into JAX's PyTree layout, allowing structured datasets to pass seamlessly through =jit=, =vmap=, and automatic differentiation boundaries. Pandas-labelled tensors remain available at the I/O boundary (load, save, summarize) via the workspace's =jax-pandas-bridge= utilities; they do not enter the hot path.

** Axis Specifications and Haliax Integration
Dimensions are promoted from implicit integer indices to first-class compile-time constants. We define the foundational axis taxonomy for any estimation instance:

#+BEGIN_SRC python
import haliax as ha

Params       = ha.Axis("parameters",      size=K)
ParamsDual   = ha.Axis("parameters_dual", size=K)
Moments      = ha.Axis("moments",         size=M)
MomentsDual  = ha.Axis("moments_dual",    size=M)
Obs          = ha.Axis("observations",    size=N)
#+END_SRC

The =Dual= axes carry the /same size/ as their primary counterparts and are used as the second index of symmetric matrices (\(\Lambda\), \(\mathcal{I}\), parameter covariances). Naming both indices of a symmetric matrix with the same string causes =ha.dot= to alias the contraction and silently produce wrong results --- the dual-name convention prevents that pitfall.

The structural components yield outputs mapped across these formal dimensions. The Sample Jacobian is typed natively as a two-dimensional named array:
\[ G_X : \Theta \to \text{ha.NamedArray}[\text{Moments}, \text{Params}] \]

The weighting matrix is typed as:
\[ \Lambda_X : \Theta \to \text{ha.NamedArray}[\text{Moments}, \text{MomentsDual}] \]

Computing the sample information metric requires renaming the duplicated axes of \(G\) before contraction:
\[ \tilde G_X = G_X.\text{rename}(\{\text{Moments}: \text{MomentsDual},\; \text{Params}: \text{ParamsDual}\}) \]
\[ \mathcal{I}_X(\hat\theta) = \text{ha.dot}(\text{Moments},\, G_X,\, \text{ha.dot}(\text{MomentsDual},\, \Lambda_X,\, \tilde G_X)) \]
producing a =NamedArray[Params, ParamsDual]= naturally, without further renaming.

Changing \(K\), \(M\), or \(N\) between runs triggers JIT recompilation; JAX's shape-signature cache amortises the cost across repeated estimations at the same dimensions, so the recompile bites only at the start of a parameter / moment sweep.

** Labeled Missingness Masks as Tensor Fields
Rather than relying on floating-point =NaN= sentinels that contaminate the automatic differentiation tape, the missingness layout is captured as a dedicated boolean structure mapped directly over the named axes:
\[ \text{mask} \in \text{ha.NamedArray}[\text{Obs}, \text{Moments}, \text{bool}] \]
The =EmpiricalCovarianceStrategy= leverages this mask to execute coordinate-wise element selection entirely within compiled tensor primitives, enabling JAX to perform highly parallelized data filtering across SIMD lanes or hardware accelerators without dropping out of the execution graph.

** Structured Egress and Metadata Binding
Because =haliax.NamedArray= instances and custom dataclasses decorated with =@jdc.pytree_dataclass= are fully compliant JAX PyTrees, the final outputs of the estimation loop require zero reconstruction or mapping steps. The =InferenceEngine= returns parameter arrays, covariance matrices, and overidentification statistics that inherently carry their coordinate strings, producing self-documenting diagnostic footprints.
